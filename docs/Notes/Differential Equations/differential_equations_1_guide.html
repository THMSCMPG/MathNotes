Population Model with Birth and Death

The standard population model \(\frac{dP}{dt} = kP\) only considers growth. Create a model that accounts for both birth and death rates, where both are proportional to the population. Use constants \(k_1\) for birth rate and \(k_2\) for death rate.

Approach 1: Verbal Reasoning

1
Understand the Rates

Birth rate: "proportional to population" ‚Üí \(k_1 P\)

Death rate: "proportional to population" ‚Üí \(k_2 P\)

2
Net Change = Births ‚àí Deaths

Population increases from births and decreases from deaths

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = \text{(rate of births)} - \text{(rate of deaths)}$$
3
Write the Model
$$\frac{dP}{dt} = k_1 P - k_2 P$$
Notice this can be factored: \(\frac{dP}{dt} = (k_1 - k_2)P\). If \(k_1 > k_2\), population grows; if \(k_1 < k_2\), it declines!

Approach 2: Rate Analysis

Key Concept: Proportionality

"Rate is proportional to X" means: Rate = (constant) √ó X
1
Identify Each Component
  • Birth rate ‚àù P ‚Üí Birth rate = \(k_1 P\) (people/time)
  • Death rate ‚àù P ‚Üí Death rate = \(k_2 P\) (people/time)
2
Apply the Net Rate Principle

The rate of change of population equals the difference between inflow and outflow:

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = \text{inflow} - \text{outflow} = k_1 P - k_2 P$$
Don't confuse proportionality with equality! "Proportional to P" means multiply by a constant, not "equals P."

Approach 3: Net Rate Method

1
Define Net Rate

Net rate = Total change in population per unit time

This equals births minus deaths (both occurring simultaneously)

2
Express Mathematically
$$\frac{dP}{dt} = (\text{birth rate}) - (\text{death rate}) = k_1 P - k_2 P$$
3
Simplify (Optional)
$$\frac{dP}{dt} = (k_1 - k_2)P = kP \quad \text{where } k = k_1 - k_2$$

This shows the model is actually the same form as the simple exponential model, but with an effective rate \(k\) that can be positive or negative!

Innovation Diffusion Model

A technological innovation spreads through a community of \(n\) people. The rate of adoption is jointly proportional to: (1) the number who have adopted it, and (2) the number who haven't. Initially, one person adopts. Find \(\frac{dx}{dt}\) and \(x(0)\).

Approach 1: Logistic Reasoning

Key Insight: Spread Requires Contact

Innovations spread when adopters meet non-adopters. The rate depends on both groups!
1
Define Variables
  • \(x(t)\) = number who have adopted
  • \(n - x(t)\) = number who haven't adopted
  • \(n\) = total population (fixed)
2
Joint Proportionality

"Jointly proportional to A and B" means: Rate = k ¬∑ A ¬∑ B

$$\frac{dx}{dt} = k \cdot x \cdot (n - x)$$
3
Initial Condition

"Initially one person adopts" means at \(t = 0\), \(x = 1\):

$$x(0) = 1$$
This is the famous logistic equation! It models many real-world phenomena: disease spread, rumors, technology adoption, population growth with carrying capacity.

Approach 2: Contact Model

1
Think About Encounters

For the innovation to spread, an adopter must encounter a non-adopter

Number of possible encounters ‚âà (adopters) √ó (non-adopters)

2
Set Up the Rate

Rate of new adoptions = \(k\) √ó (probability of encounter)

$$\frac{dx}{dt} = k \cdot x(t) \cdot [n - x(t)]$$

where \(k > 0\) captures the "effectiveness" of each encounter

3
Add Initial State
$$x(0) = 1$$
Make sure to use \((n - x)\) not just \(n\). The non-adopters decrease over time!

Approach 3: Standard Form

1
Translate "Jointly Proportional"

Jointly proportional to X and Y ‚Üí Rate = k¬∑X¬∑Y

Here: X = \(x\), Y = \((n-x)\)

2
Write the Differential Equation
$$\boxed{\frac{dx}{dt} = kx(n-x)}$$

This can also be written as: \(\frac{dx}{dt} = knx - kx^2\)

3
State Initial Condition
$$\boxed{x(0) = 1}$$
Notice: When \(x\) is small, \((n-x) ‚âà n\), so \(\frac{dx}{dt} ‚âà knx\) (exponential). When \(x ‚âà n\), \(\frac{dx}{dt} ‚âà 0\) (saturation). This creates the characteristic S-curve!

Mixing Tank Problem

A tank initially holds 700 gallons of water with 50 pounds of salt dissolved. Brine (2 lb/gal concentration) enters at 3 gal/min. The well-mixed solution exits at 2 gal/min. Find \(\frac{dA}{dt}\) in lb/min.

Approach 1: Rate In - Rate Out

Fundamental Mixing Principle

$$\frac{dA}{dt} = \text{(rate salt in)} - \text{(rate salt out)}$$
1
Calculate Rate In

Salt enters at: (concentration) √ó (flow rate)

$$\text{Rate in} = 2 \,\text{lb/gal} \times 3\, \text{gal/min} = 6\, \text{lb/min}$$
2
Calculate Rate Out

First, find the concentration in tank at time t:

Volume at time t: \(V(t) = 700 + (3-2)t = 700 + t\) gallons

Concentration: \(\frac{A(t)}{700+t}\) lb/gal

$$\text{Rate out} = \frac{A(t)}{700+t} \times 2 = \frac{2A}{700+t}\, \text{lb/min}$$
3
Write the Differential Equation
$$\boxed{\frac{dA}{dt} = 6 - \frac{2A}{700+t}}$$
The volume changes over time because inflow ‚â† outflow! Always track the volume when rates differ.

Approach 2: Balance Principle

1
Set Up the Balance

Change in salt = (salt added) - (salt removed)

2
Salt Added Per Unit Time

Entering solution: 2 lb/gal at 3 gal/min

$$\text{Salt in} = 2 \times 3 = 6\, \text{lb/min}$$
3
Salt Removed Per Unit Time

Key: Volume increases over time!

At time t: Volume = 700 + (3-2)t = 700 + t

Concentration in tank: A/(700+t) lb/gal

Leaving at 2 gal/min:

$$\text{Salt out} = \frac{A}{700+t} \times 2 = \frac{2A}{700+t}\, \text{lb/min}$$
4
Form the Equation
$$\frac{dA}{dt} = 6 - \frac{2A}{700+t}$$
Don't use 700 as a constant for volume! Since inflow > outflow, the tank is filling up.

Approach 3: Detailed Analysis

1
Organize Given Information
  • Initial volume: 700 gal
  • Initial salt: 50 lb
  • Inflow rate: 3 gal/min
  • Outflow rate: 2 gal/min
  • Incoming concentration: 2 lb/gal
2
Track Volume Change

Net flow = inflow - outflow = 3 - 2 = 1 gal/min

$$V(t) = 700 + 1 \cdot t = 700 + t\, \text{gallons}$$
3
Calculate Salt Flow Rates

Inflow:

$$\text{Salt rate in} = (\text{concentration})(\text{flow}) = (2)(3) = 6\, \text{lb/min}$$

Outflow:

Current concentration = A(t)/(700+t)

$$\text{Salt rate out} = \left(\frac{A}{700+t}\right)(2) = \frac{2A}{700+t}\, \text{lb/min}$$
4
Assemble the Model
$$\boxed{\frac{dA}{dt} = 6 - \frac{2A}{700+t}\, \text{lb/min}}$$
This is a linear first-order ODE. The volume in the denominator makes it time-varying!

RC Circuit - Capacitor Charging

A series circuit has a resistor (resistance R) and a capacitor (capacitance C). An impressed voltage E(t) is applied. Find the differential equation for the charge q(t) on the capacitor.

Approach 1: Kirchhoff's Voltage Law

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)

The sum of voltage drops around a closed loop equals the impressed voltage.
1
Identify Voltage Drops
  • Across resistor: \(V_R = iR\) where \(i = \frac{dq}{dt}\)
  • Across capacitor: \(V_C = \frac{q}{C}\)
2
Apply KVL

Sum of drops = impressed voltage:

$$iR + \frac{q}{C} = E(t)$$
3
Express in Terms of q

Since \(i = \frac{dq}{dt}\):

$$R\frac{dq}{dt} + \frac{q}{C} = E(t)$$

Solving for \(\frac{dq}{dt}\):

$$\boxed{\frac{dq}{dt} = \frac{1}{R}\left(E(t) - \frac{q}{C}\right)}$$
Alternative form: \(\frac{dq}{dt} = \frac{E(t)}{R} - \frac{q}{RC}\)

Approach 2: Component Analysis

1
Resistor Behavior

Ohm's Law: \(V = iR\)

Current through resistor: \(i = \frac{dq}{dt}\)

$$V_R = R\frac{dq}{dt}$$
2
Capacitor Behavior

Relationship between charge and voltage:

$$q = CV \quad \Rightarrow \quad V_C = \frac{q}{C}$$
3
Conservation of Energy

Voltage source = sum of voltage drops:

$$E(t) = V_R + V_C = R\frac{dq}{dt} + \frac{q}{C}$$
4
Solve for dq/dt
$$\boxed{\frac{dq}{dt} = \frac{E(t) - q/C}{R}}$$

Approach 3: Circuit Theory Derivation

1
Fundamental Relationships

For a series RC circuit:

  • Current is same everywhere: \(i = \frac{dq}{dt}\)
  • Voltages add: \(E(t) = V_R + V_C\)
2
Express Each Voltage

Resistor (Ohm's law): \(V_R = iR = R\frac{dq}{dt}\)

Capacitor (definition): \(V_C = \frac{q}{C}\)

3
Combine and Rearrange
$$E(t) = R\frac{dq}{dt} + \frac{q}{C}$$ $$R\frac{dq}{dt} = E(t) - \frac{q}{C}$$ $$\boxed{\frac{dq}{dt} = \frac{1}{R}\left(E - \frac{q}{C}\right)}$$
Don't confuse q (charge) with Q (sometimes used for heat). Always check your notation!
The time constant is \(\tau = RC\). This determines how fast the capacitor charges!

Tractrix Curve - Waterskier Problem

A motorboat at the origin moves along the positive x-axis, pulling a waterskier on a taut rope of length \(a\). The skier starts at (0, a). The rope is always tangent to the path C. Find a differential equation for the path using the concept of slope.

Approach 1: Geometric Method

Key Insight

The rope is tangent to the curve and has length \(a\). The boat is at \((x, 0)\) when the skier is at \((x, y)\).
1
Set Up the Geometry

Skier at point P(x, y)

Boat at point B on x-axis

Rope: line from B to P, length = a, tangent to curve at P

2
Use Slope of Tangent

The rope has slope \(\frac{dy}{dx} = y'\)

If boat is at \((x_B, 0)\), the slope is also:

$$y' = \frac{y - 0}{x - x_B} = \frac{y}{x - x_B}$$
3
Use Rope Length

Distance from boat to skier = a:

$$\sqrt{(x - x_B)^2 + y^2} = a$$ $$(x - x_B)^2 + y^2 = a^2$$ $$x - x_B = -\sqrt{a^2 - y^2}$$

(Negative because boat is ahead: \(x_B > x\))

4
Combine to Get y'
$$y' = \frac{y}{x - x_B} = \frac{y}{-\sqrt{a^2 - y^2}}$$ $$\boxed{y' = -\frac{y}{\sqrt{a^2 - y^2}}}$$
The negative sign indicates the curve is decreasing (skier moving down as pulled forward).

Approach 2: Tangent Line Analysis

1
Identify the Tangent

At point (x, y), the tangent line (rope) extends to the x-axis

Length of this tangent segment = a

2
Find Tangent Components

Horizontal component: \(x - x_B\)

Vertical component: \(y - 0 = y\)

Length: \(\sqrt{(x-x_B)^2 + y^2} = a\)

3
Relate to Slope

Slope of rope = \(\frac{y}{x - x_B}\)

But this equals \(y'\), the derivative!

From length constraint:

$$(x - x_B)^2 = a^2 - y^2$$ $$x - x_B = \pm\sqrt{a^2 - y^2}$$

Since boat is ahead, choose negative:

$$y' = \frac{y}{-\sqrt{a^2 - y^2}} = -\frac{y}{\sqrt{a^2 - y^2}}$$

Approach 3: Pythagorean Approach

1
Draw the Right Triangle

Vertices: Skier (x, y), Boat (\(x_B\), 0), Point (x, 0)

Hypotenuse (rope): length a

Legs: horizontal \(|x_B - x|\), vertical \(y\)

2
Apply Pythagorean Theorem
$$(x_B - x)^2 + y^2 = a^2$$

Solving for horizontal leg:

$$x_B - x = \sqrt{a^2 - y^2}$$
3
Calculate Slope

The rope slope from skier down to boat:

$$y' = \frac{0 - y}{x_B - x} = \frac{-y}{\sqrt{a^2 - y^2}}$$ $$\boxed{y' = -\frac{y}{\sqrt{a^2 - y^2}}}$$
Be careful with signs! The boat is at a greater x-coordinate, so \(x_B > x\).

Exponential Growth Rate Calculation

Given \(\frac{dP}{dt} = 0.15P(t)\) for a cell culture (in millions), find how fast the culture is growing when \(P = 2\) million cells.

Approach 1: Direct Substitution

1
Understand the Question

"How fast is the culture growing?" asks for \(\frac{dP}{dt}\)

"When the size reaches 2 million" means \(P = 2\)

2
Use the Given Equation
$$\frac{dP}{dt} = 0.15P$$
3
Substitute P = 2
$$\frac{dP}{dt} = 0.15 \times 2 = 0.3$$
4
State Answer with Units
$$\boxed{0.3 \text{ million cells per hour}}$$

Or equivalently: 300,000 cells per hour

The rate is proportional to the population. Bigger population ‚Üí faster growth!

Approach 2: Rate Interpretation

Understanding \(\frac{dP}{dt}\)

This is the instantaneous rate of change of population with respect to time.
1
Interpret the Model

\(\frac{dP}{dt} = 0.15P\) means:

  • Growth rate is proportional to current population
  • Constant of proportionality: 0.15 per hour
2
Evaluate at P = 2

When population = 2 million:

$$\frac{dP}{dt}\bigg|_{P=2} = 0.15(2) = 0.3$$
3
Interpret the Result

At this instant, the culture is growing at:

$$\boxed{0.3 \text{ million cells/hour} = 300{,}000 \text{ cells/hour}}$$
This means: "right now, the population is increasing by 0.3 million cells every hour"

Approach 3: Percent Growth Rate

1
Recognize Exponential Form

The equation \(\frac{dP}{dt} = 0.15P\) is exponential growth

0.15 = 15% per hour growth rate

2
Calculate Absolute Rate

15% of 2 million cells:

$$0.15 \times 2 = 0.3 \text{ million cells/hour}$$
3
Final Answer
$$\boxed{0.3 \text{ million cells per hour}}$$

Perspective

At P = 1 million: rate = 0.15 million/hr

At P = 2 million: rate = 0.30 million/hr

At P = 4 million: rate = 0.60 million/hr

Growth accelerates as population increases!
