1

Verifying Subspace Properties

Determine whether \(W = \{(x, y, z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 : x + 2y - z = 0\}\) is a subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

Approach 1: Direct Verification

1
Check if Zero Vector is in W

For \(\mathbf{0} = (0, 0, 0)\), verify the equation:

$$0 + 2(0) - 0 = 0 \quad \checkmark$$

The zero vector satisfies the equation, so \(\mathbf{0} \in W\).

2
Check Closure Under Addition

Let \(\mathbf{u} = (x_1, y_1, z_1)\) and \(\mathbf{v} = (x_2, y_2, z_2)\) be in \(W\).

This means:

$$x_1 + 2y_1 - z_1 = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad x_2 + 2y_2 - z_2 = 0$$

For \(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} = (x_1 + x_2, y_1 + y_2, z_1 + z_2)\):

$$(x_1 + x_2) + 2(y_1 + y_2) - (z_1 + z_2)$$ $$= (x_1 + 2y_1 - z_1) + (x_2 + 2y_2 - z_2) = 0 + 0 = 0 \quad \checkmark$$
3
Check Closure Under Scalar Multiplication

Let \(c \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(\mathbf{u} = (x, y, z) \in W\) with \(x + 2y - z = 0\).

For \(c\mathbf{u} = (cx, cy, cz)\):

$$cx + 2(cy) - cz = c(x + 2y - z) = c \cdot 0 = 0 \quad \checkmark$$
4
Conclusion
$$\boxed{W \text{ is a subspace of } \mathbb{R}^3}$$
All three subspace conditions are satisfied! This systematic verification is the most straightforward approach.

Approach 2: Null Space Approach

Key Insight

The set \(W\) can be written as the solution set of a homogeneous linear equation, which is the null space of a matrix.
1
Express as Matrix Equation

The equation \(x + 2y - z = 0\) can be written as:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} = 0$$

So \(W = \text{Null}(A)\) where \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & -1 \end{bmatrix}\).

2
Apply Null Space Theorem

The null space of any matrix is always a subspace.

This is a fundamental theorem in linear algebra that follows from:

  • \(A\mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}\) (zero vector is in null space)
  • If \(A\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{0}\) and \(A\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\), then \(A(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{0}\)
  • If \(A\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{0}\), then \(A(c\mathbf{u}) = cA\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{0}\)
3
Conclusion
$$\boxed{W = \text{Null}(A) \text{ is a subspace of } \mathbb{R}^3}$$
This approach is powerful! Any solution set to a homogeneous system \(A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}\) is automatically a subspace.

Approach 3: Parametric Form

1
Solve for Free Variables

From \(x + 2y - z = 0\), solve for \(x\):

$$x = -2y + z$$

So \(y\) and \(z\) are free variables.

2
Write in Parametric Vector Form

Let \(y = s\) and \(z = t\) where \(s, t \in \mathbb{R}\):

$$\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -2s + t \\ s \\ t \end{bmatrix} = s\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + t\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$
3
Recognize as Span

The set \(W\) is the span of two vectors:

$$W = \text{Span}\left\{\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\right\}$$
4
Apply Span Theorem

The span of any set of vectors is always a subspace.

$$\boxed{W \text{ is a subspace of } \mathbb{R}^3}$$

Bonus Insight

This shows that \(W\) is a 2-dimensional plane through the origin in \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

The two basis vectors \(\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) span this plane!

2

Testing Linear Independence

Determine if the vectors \(\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}\), \(\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 5 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix}\), and \(\mathbf{v}_3 = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 8 \\ 9 \end{bmatrix}\) are linearly independent.

Approach 1: Matrix Row Reduction

1
Form the Matrix

Create a matrix with the vectors as columns:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4 & 7 \\ 2 & 5 & 8 \\ 3 & 6 & 9 \end{bmatrix}$$
2
Row Reduce to Echelon Form

Apply row operations:

$$R_2 - 2R_1 \to R_2: \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4 & 7 \\ 0 & -3 & -6 \\ 3 & 6 & 9 \end{bmatrix}$$ $$R_3 - 3R_1 \to R_3: \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4 & 7 \\ 0 & -3 & -6 \\ 0 & -6 & -12 \end{bmatrix}$$ $$R_3 - 2R_2 \to R_3: \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4 & 7 \\ 0 & -3 & -6 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$
3
Count Pivot Positions

The echelon form has only 2 pivots (in columns 1 and 2).

Since we have 3 vectors but only 2 pivots, the vectors are linearly dependent.

4
Conclusion
$$\boxed{\text{The vectors are LINEARLY DEPENDENT}}$$
For \(n\) vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^m\), they're independent iff the matrix has \(n\) pivots!

Approach 2: Determinant Method

Determinant Test

For \(n\) vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^n\), they are linearly independent if and only if \(\det(A) \neq 0\).
1
Set Up the Matrix
$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4 & 7 \\ 2 & 5 & 8 \\ 3 & 6 & 9 \end{bmatrix}$$
2
Calculate Determinant

Using cofactor expansion along the first row:

$$\det(A) = 1\begin{vmatrix} 5 & 8 \\ 6 & 9 \end{vmatrix} - 4\begin{vmatrix} 2 & 8 \\ 3 & 9 \end{vmatrix} + 7\begin{vmatrix} 2 & 5 \\ 3 & 6 \end{vmatrix}$$ $$= 1(45 - 48) - 4(18 - 24) + 7(12 - 15)$$ $$= 1(-3) - 4(-6) + 7(-3)$$ $$= -3 + 24 - 21 = 0$$
3
Apply the Test

Since \(\det(A) = 0\), the vectors are linearly dependent.

$$\boxed{\text{The vectors are LINEARLY DEPENDENT}}$$
The determinant method only works when you have exactly \(n\) vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^n\)!

Approach 3: Direct Calculation

1
Observe the Pattern

Notice that each vector follows a pattern:

$$\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 5 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{v}_3 = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 8 \\ 9 \end{bmatrix}$$

Each successive vector increases each component by 3.

2
Find a Linear Combination

Test if \(\mathbf{v}_3\) can be written as a combination of \(\mathbf{v}_1\) and \(\mathbf{v}_2\):

Try: \(\mathbf{v}_3 = c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\mathbf{v}_2\)

$$\begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 8 \\ 9 \end{bmatrix} = c_1\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} + c_2\begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 5 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix}$$
3
Solve the System

From the first component: \(c_1 + 4c_2 = 7\)

From the second component: \(2c_1 + 5c_2 = 8\)

Solving: \(c_1 = -1\) and \(c_2 = 2\)

Verify with third component: \(3(-1) + 6(2) = -3 + 12 = 9\) ‚úì

4
Conclusion

We found: \(\mathbf{v}_3 = -\mathbf{v}_1 + 2\mathbf{v}_2\)

$$\boxed{\text{The vectors are LINEARLY DEPENDENT}}$$

Geometric Interpretation

All three vectors lie in the same plane through the origin in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)!

Finding a Basis for a Subspace

Find a basis for the subspace \(W = \{(x, y, z, w) \in \mathbb{R}^4 : x - y + 2z = 0 \text{ and } 2x + y - w = 0\}\).

Approach 1: Parametric Solution

1
Identify the System
$$\begin{cases} x - y + 2z = 0 \\ 2x + y - w = 0 \end{cases}$$

We have 4 variables and 2 equations, so expect \(4 - 2 = 2\) free variables.

2
Solve for Dependent Variables

From equation 1: \(x = y - 2z\)

Substitute into equation 2:

$$2(y - 2z) + y - w = 0$$ $$3y - 4z - w = 0$$ $$w = 3y - 4z$$

Let \(y = s\) and \(z = t\) (free variables).

3
Write in Vector Form
$$\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \\ w \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} s - 2t \\ s \\ t \\ 3s - 4t \end{bmatrix} = s\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} + t\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 0 \\ 1 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix}$$
4
Identify the Basis

A basis for \(W\) is:

$$\boxed{\left\{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 0 \\ 1 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix}\right\}}$$

Dimension of \(W\) is 2.

Approach 2: Matrix Null Space

1
Write as Matrix Equation
$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 & 2 & 0 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \\ w \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

So \(W = \text{Null}(A)\) where \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 & 2 & 0 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}\).

2
Row Reduce the Matrix
$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 & 2 & 0 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_2 - 2R_1} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & -4 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$$
3
Identify Pivot and Free Variables

Pivot columns: 1 and 2 (corresponding to \(x\) and \(y\))

Free columns: 3 and 4 (corresponding to \(z\) and \(w\))

From reduced form:

  • \(x = y - 2z\)
  • \(3y = 4z + w\), so \(y\) can be chosen freely once \(z\) and \(w\) are set
4
Find Basis Vectors

Set \(z = 1, w = 0\): gives \(\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 0 \\ 1 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix}\)

Set \(z = 0, w = 3\): gives \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}\)

$$\boxed{\text{Basis: } \left\{\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 0 \\ 1 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}\right\}}$$

Approach 3: Row Reduction to RREF

1
Form Augmented Matrix
$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 & 2 & 0 & | & 0 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 & -1 & | & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$
2
Reduce to RREF
$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 & 2 & 0 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_2 - 2R_1}$$ $$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & -4 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_2/3}$$ $$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -4/3 & -1/3 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_1 + R_2}$$ $$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2/3 & -1/3 \\ 0 & 1 & -4/3 & -1/3 \end{bmatrix}$$
3
Express in Terms of Free Variables

From RREF:

$$x = -\frac{2}{3}z + \frac{1}{3}w$$ $$y = \frac{4}{3}z + \frac{1}{3}w$$

Let \(z = 3s\) and \(w = 3t\) to eliminate fractions:

$$\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \\ w \end{bmatrix} = s\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 4 \\ 3 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + t\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}$$
4
Simplify to Get Basis

Scaling the first vector by 1/3:

$$\boxed{\text{Basis: } \left\{\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 0 \\ 1 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}\right\}}$$
Different approaches may give different-looking bases, but they span the same subspace!
Key Takeaways: Vector spaces and linear independence are foundational concepts in linear algebra. Recognizing when sets form subspaces (null spaces, spans, solution sets) and determining linear independence (row reduction, determinants, direct verification) are essential skills.
